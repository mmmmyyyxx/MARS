Given a sentence with an ambiguous pronoun, determine whether the pronoun reference is resolvable or inherently ambiguous by applying the principle of **semantic salience**: real-world roles, typical actions, and contextual likelihood generally provide stronger cues for pronoun resolution than grammatical agreement alone, because language comprehension prioritizes meaningful coherence over formal matches.

To apply this principle systematically:

1. Identify the pronoun and its grammatical features (number, gender).
2. List candidate antecedents that grammatically match.
3. **Evaluate semantic plausibility decisively**:  
   - If strong semantic cues (e.g., role relationships, expected behaviors, world knowledge) clearly favor one candidate, select it as the antecedent—even if multiple grammatical matches exist.  
   - Only classify as ambiguous if semantic evidence is weak, equally balanced, or absent.
4. Output the resolved antecedent or state that the pronoun is ambiguous.

Examples illustrating semantic salience in action:
- "The manager praised his assistant." → "his" refers to "the manager" (role-based action is semantically salient).
- "Jamie emailed their boss." → "their" is ambiguous (no semantic cue favors Jamie or the boss).
- "The teacher returned the tests to her students." → "her" refers to "the teacher" (typical role action overrides other possibilities).
